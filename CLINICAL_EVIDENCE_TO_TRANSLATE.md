## INT_001: Acute Appendicitis (Grade II - Mild Appendicitis)

### supportingEvidence (FOR diagnosis):
1. Migration of pain from periumbilical to right lower quadrant is classic for appendicitis
2. Positive McBurney's point tenderness and Rovsing's sign strongly suggest appendiceal inflammation
3. WBC 12.8 x10³/μL elevated, consistent with acute inflammatory process
4. CT shows fluid-filled appendix 8mm diameter (>6mm diagnostic threshold)
5. Enhancing appendiceal wall thickening confirms active inflammation
6. CRP 0.95 mg/dL matches BJR 2011 Grade II range (0.99±1.63 mg/dL)

### clinicalReasoning:
The patient presents with classic appendicitis progression: initial periumbilical pain (visceral innervation) migrating to right lower quadrant (parietal peritoneum involvement). Physical exam findings of McBurney's point tenderness with positive Rovsing's sign have high positive predictive value. Laboratory markers show WBC and CRP levels precisely matching BJR 2011 Grade II appendicitis cohort. CT imaging confirms appendiceal diameter >6mm with wall enhancement, meeting diagnostic criteria for mild appendicitis without perforation.

### contrastiveEvidence.supporting:
1. Appendiceal diameter 8mm exceeds 6mm diagnostic threshold (BJR 2011 criteria)
2. Enhancing wall thickening visible on CT confirms active inflammation
3. WBC 12.8 x10³/μL matches BJR 2011 Grade II mean (12.97±3.73)
4. Anorexia and nausea typical for appendicitis (98% sensitive)

### contrastiveEvidence.challenging:
1. CRP only mildly elevated (0.95 mg/dL) - perforation would show CRP >8 mg/dL (BJR 2011 Grade IV)
2. Only subtle periappendiceal stranding - moderate-severe stranding would suggest Grade III
3. No abscess or phlegmon formation - rules out complicated appendicitis
4. Fever only 38.5°C - perforated appendicitis typically >39°C

## INT_002: Ascending Cholangitis

2. Pericholecystic fluid indicates acute inflammation
3. Positive sonographic Murphy's sign strongly suggests gallbladder pathology
4. WBC markedly elevated (16.2 x10³/μL) consistent with acute bacterial infection
5. Multiple gallstones present as nidus for inflammation

### clinicalReasoning:

Laboratory shows elevated WBC and liver enzymes. Common bile duct at 7mm is at upper limit of normal. ### contrastiveEvidence.supporting:
1. Positive Murphy's sign indicates gallbladder inflammation
2. Gallbladder wall thickening >3mm confirms cholecystitis
3. Pericholecystic fluid supports acute inflammatory process
4. History of prior similar episode suggests chronic gallstone disease

### contrastiveEvidence.challenging:

3. Dark urine indicates conjugated hyperbilirubinemia (cholestatic pattern)

5. Shaking chills (Reynold's pentad component) more specific for cholangitis than cholecystitis
6. Hypotension (94/62) and tachycardia (116) suggest systemic infection beyond gallbladder

## INT_005: Type A Aortic Dissection Complicated by Mesenteric Ischemia

### supportingEvidence (FOR diagnosis):
1. Hypotension (96/45 mmHg) with relative bradycardia (61 bpm) suggests cardiovascular catastrophe
2. Faint pulses in all extremities indicates poor perfusion from aortic pathology
3. Lactate 9.6→10.7 mmol/L critically elevated, diagnostic for mesenteric ischemia
4. Abdominal pain out of proportion to exam classic for mesenteric ischemia
5. Risk factors present: hypertension, cocaine use (vasoconstrictor causing catecholamine surge)
6. CTA confirms aortic dissection with true lumen compression at mesenteric vessels
7. Multi-organ failure: renal (Cr 6.67), hepatic (AST 3361, ALT 1546), bowel (lactate 10.7)

### clinicalReasoning:
This is a catastrophic presentation of Type A aortic dissection with mesenteric malperfusion, documented in Cureus 2024 case report. Patient has multiple risk factors: hypertension and cocaine use (causes catecholamine surge and blood pressure spike that can precipitate intimal tear). The combination of severe abdominal pain, altered mental status, hypotension with faint pulses, and critically elevated lactate (>9 mmol/L) indicates mesenteric ischemia from aortic dissection involving visceral vessels. CTA confirms dissection flap at celiac/SMA level with bowel ischemia. Multi-organ failure (renal, hepatic, intestinal) reflects widespread malperfusion - occurs in 30% of aortic dissections per Cureus source.

### contrastiveEvidence.supporting:
1. Aortic dissection confirmed on CTA with flap at mesenteric vessel level (Cureus 2024 source)
2. Lactate 10.7 mmol/L critically elevated - diagnostic for mesenteric ischemia per source
3. Multi-organ failure pattern (renal Cr 6.67, hepatic transaminases >3000) from malperfusion
4. Risk factors: HTN + cocaine use documented in Cureus 2024 as predisposing factors
5. Hypotension + faint pulses + bradycardia = classic dissection triad

### contrastiveEvidence.challenging:
1. Initial chest X-ray showed no widened mediastinum - but Cureus source notes this has declined sensitivity (70%)
2. Patient appears to have primarily abdominal symptoms, not chest pain - but 30% of dissections present with malperfusion symptoms
3. Age 57 slightly younger than typical (peak 50-65 per source) but cocaine use is risk factor for younger presentations

## INT_010: Acute Appendicitis Grade III (Severe Appendicitis with Localized Peritonitis, No Perforation)

### supportingEvidence (FOR diagnosis):
1. Severe clinical presentation with high fever (39.1°C) and marked peritoneal signs
2. WBC 13.5 consistent with severe infection
3. Elevated CRP 5.8 mg/dL suggests significant inflammation
4. Appendix 10mm diameter significantly enlarged (>6mm abnormal)
5. Severe periappendiceal fat stranding extending to paracolic gutter
6. Free fluid in pelvis suggests severe inflammatory response

### clinicalReasoning:
Patient presents with severe acute appendicitis: marked RLQ pain, high fever, peritoneal signs, elevated WBC/CRP. CT shows severely inflamed appendix (10mm) with extensive periappendiceal stranding and free fluid. These findings raise concern for perforation (Grade IV). However, BJR 2011 grading system is explicit: Grade IV requires visible appendiceal wall defect with phlegmon or abscess formation. Careful review of CT shows appendiceal wall appears intact with no visible defect. Free fluid alone is not diagnostic of perforation - can occur with severe localized peritonitis (Grade III). CRP 5.8 mg/dL is between Grade III (mean 2.39) and Grade IV (mean 8.10), consistent with severe Grade III rather than perforated Grade IV. Correct diagnosis: Grade III (severe appendicitis with localized peritonitis, no perforation).

### contrastiveEvidence.supporting:
1. Severe clinical presentation: high fever 39.1°C, marked peritoneal signs
2. Elevated CRP 5.8 mg/dL (Grade IV mean 8.10 per BJR 2011)
3. Extensive periappendiceal stranding suggests severe inflammation
4. Free fluid in pelvis often seen with perforation
5. Prolonged symptom duration (36 hours) increases perforation risk
6. Severe pain (9/10) and guarding suggest peritonitis

### contrastiveEvidence.challenging:
1. NO appendiceal wall defect visible on CT - BJR 2011 requires this for Grade IV diagnosis
2. NO phlegmon or abscess formation - BJR 2011 requires wall defect + phlegmon/abscess for Grade IV
3. Appendiceal wall appears INTACT on imaging - this rules out perforation
4. CRP 5.8 is BELOW Grade IV mean (8.10±6.90 per BJR 2011) - more consistent with severe Grade III
5. Free fluid can occur with severe localized peritonitis (Grade III) without perforation
6. BJR 2011 CT criteria: Grade III = severe stranding + NO perforation. Grade IV = wall defect + phlegmon/abscess

## INT_014: Paralytic Ileus (Postoperative)

### supportingEvidence (FOR diagnosis):
1. Bowel dilation present throughout small and large bowel
2. Post-operative day 3 is typical timeframe for post-op ileus
3. No passage of bowel movement or gas since surgery
4. Hypoactive bowel sounds consistent with ileus
5. Air-fluid levels visible on imaging

### clinicalReasoning:
Patient presents with post-operative bowel dysfunction 3 days after laparoscopic cholecystectomy. Imaging shows diffuse bowel dilation involving both small and large bowel. The AI recommendation of 'Small Bowel Obstruction' is a sensible foil because bowel IS dilated. However, the KEY DIFFERENTIATOR is the ABSENCE of a transition point. Mechanical SBO requires a transition point where bowel abruptly changes from dilated to collapsed (as seen in case report and INT_012). This case shows GENERALIZED dilation without transition - pathognomonic for paralytic ileus, not mechanical obstruction. Normal labs and recent surgery confirm postoperative ileus diagnosis.

### contrastiveEvidence.supporting:
1. Bowel dilation visible throughout abdomen
2. No BM/gas post-operatively mimics mechanical obstruction symptoms
3. Air-fluid levels can occur in both ileus and obstruction

### contrastiveEvidence.challenging:
1. NO transition point on CT - this is THE defining feature that distinguishes ileus from mechanical SBO
2. DIFFUSE dilation of small AND large bowel - mechanical SBO shows collapsed bowel distal to obstruction
3. Post-op day 3 is classic timeframe for paralytic ileus (not adhesive SBO which occurs later)
4. Normal lactate (1.2 mmol/L) - mechanical obstruction often shows elevated lactate
5. Bowel enhances normally - no ischemia. Strangulated SBO would show abnormal enhancement
6. Case report criteria: SBO requires 'transition point visible' - NOT present here
